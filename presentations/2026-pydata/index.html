data structure → visual structure → reader
The key idea of this talk is that when we're designing a graphic, we're doing a lot more than just picking a chart type — we're creating a visual structure for our readers to navigate. The way we arrange shapes and the colors we assign affect what our readers see first, what comparisons they make, and what story they take away.

In this talk you're going to see many examples of mapping data structures to visual structures to reader experiences. And through that process I'm gonna show you how thinking structurally frees you from this useful, but rigid lens of chart types and black-and-white design rules like banning pie charts, and helps you think more precisely and creatively about visualization.

To talk about visual structure precisely, we need a language.

GoFish
A compositional language for visual structure
open source (MIT-licensed), TypeScript with Python bindings (quickly catching up to TS)
See papers: UIST 2024, VIS 2025
And to talk about visual structure precisely, we're gonna want a language. I've spent my PhD studying how visual structure conveys meaning and designing better libraries to take advantage of it.

The culmination of that work is GoFish, a compositional language for visual structure.

GoFish is based on Gestalt grouping principles

containment
connection
similar attribute
alignment
proximity / uniform density
The big idea in GoFish is to identify these primitives of visual structure, most often called Gestalt grouping principles, and turn them in "graphical operators" that we can manipulate directly in code. In fact with the grouped bar charts, similar attribute and spatial proximity were driving the differences in our readings of those charts. They made different visual "queries" easier or harder.

sorting keys

→
Same structure. One new operator: derive() sorts bars by count within each stack before placing them. Small change, significant effect on the ribbon that follows.

derive() to sort

chart(data).flow(
    spread("lake",   dir="x"),
    derive(lambda d: sorted(d, key="count")),
    stack("species", dir="y"),
).mark(
    rect(h="count", fill="species")
)

Queries:

  • greatest/least lake total
  • pairwise lake comparisons

  • part-to-whole species comparisons (e.g., what % of Lake A is Bass?)
  • pairwise comparisons of the same species across lakes
Because we've separated our chart's construction into four steps --- data, spread, stack, and rect --- we have a lot more control over when and how data transforms happen than we typically do in the GoG. As a result, we can just insert a special "derive" operator into our flow, which will let us write any data transformation we want after the spread but before the stacking. So now I can pretty easily tell that trout is the most abundant in Lake A, Catfish in Lake B, and Salmon in the rest. But it's harder to tell how these values are changing over time.

This is composition — nesting one query-affording structure inside another. It's the same idea as faceting: spreading a chart-making function. Pie affords proportion queries easily; magnitude queries are hard because arc angle is a weaker encoding than length. We took one dataset and made a series of deliberate structural moves.

Each move changed what was easy or hard to see.

We didn't have to pick from a menu of charts... though those are useful shorthands for common structures!

operators map data types to Gestalt relations

Operator Data type Gestalt relation
spread discrete alignment & uniform density
stack partitioned continuous part-to-whole
scatter continuous × continuous none — no discrete grouping
area / line / ribbon continuous or contiguous connection
table discrete × discrete uniform density
Each operator corresponds to a specific Gestalt principle. Spread creates alignment and uniform density across discrete categories. Stack creates part-to-whole contiguity. Area/line/ribbon use element connectedness to imply continuity. Scatter deliberately avoids a discrete grouping — proximity is the only cue. Table and align/distribute are constraint-based; their Gestalt relation is also alignment and density, but the mechanism differs.

takeaways

1. Chart types are great shortcuts, but graphics are really sophisticated visual structures.

2. Changing this structure changes what your reader can and can't see in (probably) predictable ways.

3. Thinking structurally helps us move beyond familiar charts and rigid rules to designs that might better fit our data or task.

Land all three explicitly. 1: the Part 1 journey — bar, stacked, sorted, ribbon, polar — weren't different chart types, they were structural moves. 2: affordances thread — every move opens some queries and closes others. 3: the composition payoff — scatter-pie, flower, balloon weren't in any dropdown.

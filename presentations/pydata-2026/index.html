Act 2 — The Gap

The world of graphics we can reliably make is quite small

The Grammar of Graphics (ggplot, Altair, Vega-Lite) gives us a powerful but bounded typology.

It's a taxonomy of charts, not a generative grammar. When you need something outside its vocabulary, you fall off a cliff.

It helps us think about bars, lines, areas, scales, colors. But it doesn't help us reason about the structure of graphics well enough to apply that reasoning to new problems we haven't seen before.

Don't trash ggplot — it's wonderful within its bounds. The point is that those bounds exist, and they're narrower than the space of useful visual representations. 3 min mark.

Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 1 of 6

A simple bar chart

from gofish import chart, spread, rect

chart(data).flow(
    spread("lake", dir="x")
).mark(
    rect(h="count")
)

chart(data) — attach data to a builder

.flow(operators) — arrange the data

.mark(shape) — draw something

Familiar territory. Nobody's impressed yet. That's fine.

Get the API under people's fingers. Notice that spread("lake") puts one column per lake — there's no x/y coordinate assignment. GoFish infers it from the operator. 6 min mark.
Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 2 of 6

Facet it — but notice what facet actually is

# mark doesn't just take a shape.
# It takes a chart.

chart(data).flow(
    spread("lake", dir="x", spacing=48)
).mark(
    lambda data:
        chart(data)
            .flow(spread("species", dir="x"))
            .mark(rect(h="count", w=20))
)

Faceting is spreading a chart-making function.

Uses the same spread as the rectangles themselves.

You're composing charts inside charts. Obviously.

This is the conceptual hinge. In ggplot, facet_wrap is a separate concept. Here, it's just spread() with a chart-shaped mark. The outer spread and the inner spread are the same function. 9 min mark.
Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 3 of 6

Put any chart in the mark slot

from gofish import scatter, stack, clock

chart(scatter_data).flow(
    scatter("lake", x="x", y="y")
).mark(
    lambda data:
        chart(
            data[0]["collection"],
            options={"coord": clock()}
        )
        .flow(stack("species", dir="x", h=20))
        .mark(rect(w="count", fill="species"))
)

Outer chart: places lakes in space

Inner chart: draws a pie for each lake's species data

coord=clock() wraps x → angle, y → radius

Same structure as the facet. We just changed the inner chart to a pie + scatter instead of a bar. The mark slot accepts a chart builder. 12 min mark.
Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 3.5 of 6

Layer charts on top of each other

from gofish import Layer, select, circle, line

Layer([
    chart(data)
        .flow(scatter("year",
              x="miles", y="gas"))
        .mark(circle(r=4).name("points")),

    chart(select("points"))
        .mark(line(stroke="black")),
])

.name("points") — registers each node by name

select("points") — another chart inherits their positions

Useful for regression lines, annotations, references to previous layers.

Brief interlude on layering. The second chart knows nothing about miles/gas — it just connects nodes that are already placed. 14 min mark.
Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 4 of 6

Put a glyph in the mark slot

def box_and_whisker(median, q1, q3, min, max):
    return Frame([
        ConnectY([Rect(y=min), Rect(y=max)]),
        Rect(y=q1, h=q3 - q1, fill="steelblue"),
        Rect(y=median, h=1, fill="white"),
    ])

chart(salary_data).flow(
    spread("pay_grade", dir="x")
).mark(
    lambda data: box_and_whisker(
        median=data[0]["median"],
        q1=data[0]["q1"], q3=data[0]["q3"],
        min=data[0]["min"], max=data[0]["max"],
    )
)
We didn't change spread. We just replaced the rect with a richer glyph. The outer structure is identical.
The key insight: box_and_whisker is just a mark. We swap it in where rect used to be. The spread doesn't care what it's spreading. 17 min mark.
Act 3 — The Dissolve · Step 5 of 6

This starts to feel like a diagram

from gofish import Spread, For, Ellipse, Layer

Layer([
    Spread(direction="x", spacing=50, [
        Ellipse(
            w=p["radius"] * 2,
            h=p["radius"] * 2,
            fill=p["color"]
        ).name(p["name"])
        for p in planets
    ]),
    # label each planet below it
    *[Spread(direction="y", spacing=0, [
        Text(text=p["name"]),
        Ref(p["name"]),
    ]) for p in planets],
])

Same operators — Spread, Layer, named elements. But nobody calls this a "chart". The boundary has dissolved.

This is the payoff of the whole dissolve. We've been using the same operators the whole way. The difference between a chart and a diagram is just what marks you put in the slots. 21 min mark.

# Scatter + flower
.mark(lambda d: Frame(
    StackX(h=total(d)/7,
        For(d, (item, i) =>
            Petal(w=item.count, fill=colors[i])))
    , coord=polar()))

# Scatter + balloon
.mark(lambda d: Balloon(
    scale=d[0]["count"] / max_count,
    color=colors[d[0]["species"]]
))

The outer scatter operator is unchanged.

The mark slot accepts any visual structure.

There are tradeoffs in familiarity and readability. But you have latitude — and increasingly, AI generation makes complex glyphs cheaper to build.
Show the flower chart as the live demo. Mention scatter-pie was already shown. Balloon is another variant. The same data structure, same outer layout — you're choosing the glyph to fit the occasion. 25 min mark.
